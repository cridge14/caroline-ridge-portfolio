# User research 

## Target audience
While on face value, it may seem like my target audience is individuals with disabilities, that is not the case. Individuals with disabilities don't need these statistics or a description of what life is like with a disability... they live this every day! Instead, I want to focus on *non-disabled* people. I want to share a perspective with an audience who probably does not think about issues of physical accessibility, and have them shift their preconceived notions. 

For my final call to action, I want to advocate for the audience to think about instances in their everyday life where they may have an accessibility issue, and reframe their thinking to how we can make this more accessible for everyone. 

## Interview script
For my user interviews, there were a couple goals I wanted to acheive. My overall objective through this story is inform non-disabled individuals about the experiences we take for granted, but seriously upend the lives of individuals with disabilities. The three main goals I want to reach through my interviews are listed below: 

* Frame the story not as an "us-versus-them" (i.e. disabled people versus non-disabled people), but rather as a collaborative opportunity to make *everyone* better off (basically, I do not want to just tell a downer story with no possible change).
* Provide data-driven insights about the experiences of individuals with disabilities, while also having a good balance of real-life stories from people with disabilties (so a good balance between data and a story).
* Help others understand that there is still ample work to be done to make the world more accessible and equitable for people with disabilities (while also giving a solid call-to-action).

My fourth and final goal is that people learn something new! 

## Interview Questions 
In order to make sure my story is reaching these goals, I asked 5 PPM students (between the ages of 22-28, multiple genders, all abled-bodied) the following questions about my project draft. Each of the questions addressed all of the goals I hoped to achieve above. 

* After looking through this draft, what emotions did the story make you feel?
* Was there opportunity for change/ improvement based on the information provided in this story?
* (Without them looking at my draft) Please tell me back either one fact or story that you remember from my story.

I also asked one last, very general question: is there anything else you think I should know about this story? 

I believe these questions emcompassed all the goals I wanted feedback on, and would provide additional insight beyond those goals. For the emotions question, I specifically wanted to see if people were feel sad, depressed, hopeless, OR were they feeling motivated, inspired, ready for action? The goal of my story is the latter, so if my interviewees are feeling troubled, I need to shift my narrative. For the next question, I want them to directly tell me if there is a call-to-action. If they respond with "no, I didn't feel like there was" (which, as you'll read a little later on, they did exactly that), that would tell me to make that aspect of my presentation more explicit. And lastly, I wanted to put my interviewees on the spot, and regurgitate it back to me. If the majority of the audience told me a story, then I know I am leaning too heavily on the story aspect. Same with the data. However, if it's about 50-50 (half of them tell me a piece of data and half tell me a story), then I know I'm in a sweet spot. 

## Interview findings
After conducting my five interviews, a general theme came up: I focused too heavily on the story side of things, and needed to incorporate more data. I wasn't super shocked at this revelation; I tend to find more interest in lived experiences, and believe we are heavily underusing them when we make policy recommendations and decision that affect real-life people. However, there is also harm in relying *only* on lived experiences. I knew that would be something I needed to change in my final. 

As one of my interviewees stated, "...so, what am I supposed to do about this?". And I agree, it is really hard to craft this message without having the audience response, "But why is this my problem?" I need to make sure that I craft the story in the direction of here's where society is lacking in protecting people with disabilities, but there is a lot of good things, too. One thing my users didn't say, though, was that the story made them feel sad. That was good to hear- why they might not have said it made them feel happy, the genreral concensus was they felt pretty... neutral. I can work with this. 

For my last interview question, "is there anything else you think I should know about this story?", I got mixed responses. I was hit with one, "that was really good!" (which, while boosting my ego, is not super helpful for implementing feedback), and most of the others that readdressed issues that were already on my mind with the previous questions (i.e., someone brought up about how they think a couple more visualizations would be helpful), there was one answer that I didn't even think about. The interviewee told me, "Considering that you aren't an individual with a disability, how can you present this information without accidentally presenting yourself as a member of the disability community?" (for reference, only individuals that identify with a disability are considered "in" the disability community; I do not identify with a disability, so I am just an ally. This interviewee is also a heavy advocate for people with disabilities, which is why I really appreciated their perspective). I wasn't really sure how I framed that, so I needed to to really think about how I spoke about these issues. 

# Identified changes for Part III
Based on the feedback from my interviews, I made a list of a couple identifiable changes I can make for part III. 

* In the very beginning of the presentation, with the picture of my mom and sister, stating my personal connection to this issue, but making it explicitly clear I do not identify as a member of the disability community.
* Including at least 4 more visualizations that I can incorporate *with* a lived experience. For example, in the healthcare section, I can quote the story of a woman wheelchair user whose breast cancer diagnosis was delayed due to inaccessible mammogram equipment, while simultaneously showing a statistic of how many disabled women in total die from the same issue each year.
* Completely reshaping my call-to-action. I will add in multiple buttons in the bottom of the last section, "Where We Go From Here", that provide resources and ways to support individuals with disabilities in the local community. For example, there are 2 bakeries/ cafes, Bitty & Beaus in the Strip District, and Bunny's Bakes in Squirrel Hill, that specifically hire individuals with disabilities. That is an easy way to support these companies, advancing advocacy work with a wider audience.

I belive that implementing these actions will greatly refine my story, and achieve the goals that I wanted through telling this story. I am very passionate about this topic and disability justice in general, and if nothing else, I certainly hope that comes across in my presentation!
