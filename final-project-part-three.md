# Changes made since Part II
I made quite a lot of changes since Part II, due to interviewee feedback and then also feedback from the class instructors. I first decided to not include all the separate tabs for healthcare, transportation, and higher education. I thought this may make the story too convoluted, and honestly, just building the main part of my story took *way* more time than I initially intended. I still wanted to cover those topics, so I included a short section about healthcare access in the section, "Where the ADA has failed people with disabilities". 

I also changed how much of the story was just based on lived experiences/ personal stories, and how I can incorporate data into my story. The first section, "The History of the American with Disabilities Act", was just that- a history of the ADA. I didn't include any data visualizations in that section because I wanted to provide the foundation for my story. I really wanted to include real photos that were taken during the Capitol Crawl, but unfortunately, all the photos I could find were copyrighted. I think that would have been a great narrative of my story, so I am really disappointed that there weren't any images that were free to use. 

Lastly, from my objectives in Part II, I added many more data visualizations than I initially was going to, including a map of the United States that shows the prevalence of disabilities by states (we found that West Virginia and Puerto Rico has the highest prevalence, which, WV didn't surprise me, but the PR did. I would like to separately explore that, and see what contributes to that correlation). 

## The audience
For my audience, I really wanted to cater it towards people who do not have a disability. As I stated in the first part of my Shorthand story, "For disabled Americans... these are not new narratives." The whole objective of my story is to have people put themselves in someone else's shoes, and try to live through an experience that (most) people in this school/ class do not have. I also tried to include quotes from disabilitiy activists; while I can provide the data, I myself do not have a disability, so it would be unfair of me to speak on behalf of the community. I ran the project by my mom, and while she also cannot speak for the whole disability community either, having her insight/ perspectives and the quotes from lived experiences, I hope I graciously told that story. 

For my interviews, I intentionally chose people who did not have a disability. I wanted to get their feedback, see if they understood the story, and what their next steps would be based on the information I just presented them. Including the specific Pittsburgh-based restaurants that employ & support people with disabilities is also an easy way for anyone to get involved and support individuals with disabilities in the local community. I hope that some of the people who see my story will go and visit these restaurants! 

## Final design decisions
I talked a little about my design decisions in the Changes I've Made Since Part II section, but I will go a little bit more in-depth here. 

For the overall aesthetic of my presentation, I tried to keep a general light/dark color scheme, and the consistent use of orange to represent non-disabled & blue to represent disability in my visualizations. For the pictures in my story, I feel like I may have had more trouble working and finding non-copyrighted images than my peers. I really wanted specific pictures, especially of disability-rights activists, but I couldn't get any on free platforms, which really disappointed me and I personally think took away from the overall message of my story. I hope that the visualizations made up some of that gap.
